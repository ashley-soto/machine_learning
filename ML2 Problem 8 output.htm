Interpretation:

  • Rules where the LHS is empty (no specific antecedent), mean that these are simply common items that occur on their own.
  • Support: How frequently the items in the rule (LHS + RHS) appear together as a proportion of the total transactions. For example, rule 89 has 0.001 support, which indicates that only 0.1% of the transanctions contain honey and whole milk together.
  • Confidence: The proportion of transactions with the LHS that also include the RHS. For instance, in rule 89 has a 0.73 confidence, which means that 73% of the time honey is purchased also whole milk is purchased.

The first plot tells us that - Many rules with very low support but high confidence. These are rules that are very specific and apply to only a small subset of transactions but are very reliable when they do occur. - Darker red point show strong associations that are more significant than random chance. Although these rules might have low support, they could represent interesting niche patterns or complementary products in the dataset. - There are only a few rules with higher support (closer to 0.2 on the X-axis). These rules are more common across many transactions, but their confidence and lift tend to be lower, suggesting more general patterns rather than specific associations.

Second Plot: - Several rules with very high lift (up to around 30) but very low support. These represent rare item combinations that, when they do occur, are highly indicative of one another. - There is a dense cluster of rules with low support and moderate lift (around 10-15), indicating that these rules are relatively strong associations but occur in few transactions.

Third plot: - Order 1 Rules: most of these rules are concentrated on the right side with low support and low confidence. These rules typically represent single items and are not of much interest in association rule mining since they do not reveal meaningful associations. - Order 2-4 Rules: These are more complex rules with multiple items. Points are clustered closer to the left (low support), but some have relatively high confidence and can represent more interesting associations.

Confidence and Support Trade-off: Higher-order rules tend to have higher confidence because they describe more specific relationships. However, their support is often lower, meaning these rules are applicable in fewer transactions, which is why they are clustered near the left.

grocery_graph <- associations2igraph(groceryrules, associationsAsNodes = FALSE)
igraph::write_graph(grocery_graph, file='groceryrules.graphml', format = "graphml")

The graph is highly connected, indicating a complex web of associations between many items. The density suggests that there are many rules with overlapping items, making it challenging to distinguish individual relationships visually.

I applied new thresholds for lift, confidence, or support to filter out less interesting associations and reduce clutter in the graph:

  • Increased support to 0.005 since low support results in many rare rules that may not be actionable or interesting.
  • Increased confidence to 0.1 to ensure focusing on more reliable rules.
  • Lowered maximum rule length to 3 to focus on simpler associations, which are often more actionable.
